# The Day I Stopped Overthinking and Started Trusting My Gut

**Topic:** Personal development, decision-making, self-reflection

I used to be the person who made pro-con lists for everything. Should I take that job? List. Should I move cities? List. Should I even order pizza or cook dinner? You guessed it—list.

On paper, this seemed logical. Rational. Smart, even. But in reality, I was drowning in analysis paralysis. Every decision felt like a high-stakes exam I hadn't studied for, and I was terrified of getting the answer wrong.

The breaking point came on a Tuesday afternoon when I spent two hours researching the "best" running shoes, only to close my laptop feeling more confused than when I started. That's when I realized: I wasn't making better decisions by overthinking. I was just delaying them.

## The Overthinking Trap

Here's what I've learned about chronic overthinking: it's not about being thorough. It's about fear.

Fear of making the wrong choice. Fear of regretting it later. Fear of what others might think. So we collect more data, seek more opinions, and convince ourselves that *this time*, with just a little more information, we'll finally feel certain.

But certainty never comes. Because the problem isn't a lack of information—it's a lack of trust in ourselves.

I realized I had outsourced my decision-making to everyone and everything except the one person who actually had to live with the consequences: me.

## The Shift: From External Validation to Internal Clarity

The turning point wasn't dramatic. I didn't have an epiphany on a mountaintop or read a life-changing book. Instead, I stumbled onto something unexpected: AI-powered tarot readings.

I know what you're thinking. Tarot? Really? But hear me out.

I wasn't looking for mystical predictions or cosmic guidance. What caught my attention was the *structure* of it—the way it forced me to articulate what I was actually worried about, then reflect on it from different angles.

I tried [AI Divination](https://aidivination.net) on a whim, entering a question about a career decision I'd been agonizing over for weeks. The three-card reading didn't tell me what to do. Instead, it asked me to consider:

- What past experiences were influencing my current hesitation?
- What was I really afraid of in the present moment?
- What would a confident version of myself do next?

It wasn't magic. It was a mirror. And for the first time in months, I stopped looking outward for answers and started looking inward.

## Why Reflection Tools Work Better Than Advice

Here's the thing about asking for advice: people give you *their* answer, not yours. They filter your situation through their own experiences, values, and biases. Even when they mean well, their guidance is colored by who *they* are, not who *you* are.

Reflection tools—whether it's journaling, meditation, or yes, even AI tarot—work differently. They don't impose an external perspective. Instead, they help you access your own.

When I used the tarot reading, I wasn't getting someone else's opinion. I was getting a structured prompt that helped me organize my own thoughts. The cards didn't make the decision for me; they clarified what I already knew but hadn't articulated yet.

And that's when I realized: I didn't need more advice. I needed better questions.

## The Questions That Changed Everything

After that first reading, I started applying the same framework to other decisions:

**1. What's the real question here?**
Often, the question we think we're asking isn't the real one. "Should I take this job?" might actually be "Am I ready to leave my comfort zone?" Naming the real question cuts through the noise.

**2. What do I already know?**
We underestimate our own intuition. When I stopped and asked myself what I *actually* thought—not what I *should* think—the answer was usually already there.

**3. What's the worst that could happen, and could I handle it?**
Most of our overthinking is catastrophizing in disguise. When I forced myself to name the worst-case scenario, it was almost always something I could survive.

**4. What would I tell a friend in this situation?**
We're often kinder and clearer when advising others than when advising ourselves. This question helped me access that same clarity.

## From Paralysis to Action

I'm not going to pretend I've completely stopped overthinking. Old habits die hard. But I've gotten much better at recognizing when I'm stuck in analysis mode and need to shift into reflection mode.

Now, when I catch myself spiraling, I pause and ask: "What do I actually think about this?" Sometimes I use a tarot reading to structure my thoughts. Sometimes I journal. Sometimes I just sit with the question for five minutes without reaching for my phone.

The method doesn't matter as much as the mindset shift: from seeking external validation to trusting internal clarity.

## The Unexpected Benefit: Faster, Better Decisions

Here's the irony: once I stopped overthinking, I started making *better* decisions, not worse ones.

Why? Because I was no longer paralyzed by the fear of being wrong. I could make a choice, learn from it, and adjust if needed. I stopped treating every decision like a permanent, irreversible life sentence.

And the decisions I made from a place of internal clarity—rather than external pressure—felt more aligned with who I actually am. Even when they didn't work out perfectly, I didn't regret them, because they were *mine*.

## Your Turn: Try This Today

If you're stuck in overthinking mode right now, here's a simple exercise:

1. Write down the decision you're struggling with.
2. Set a timer for 5 minutes.
3. Ask yourself: "If I trusted myself completely, what would I do?"
4. Write whatever comes to mind, without editing or second-guessing.

You might be surprised by what you already know.

And you're the only one who can know that.
